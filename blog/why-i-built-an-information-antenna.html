Why I Built an Information Antenna

The problem with getting your worldview from 5 sources.

I used to scrape Reddit. A few subreddits, some keyword filters, maybe HN on top. It worked for a while. Then I realized something uncomfortable: I was building my entire understanding of the world from what a very specific group of people chose to upvote.

Reddit is great at surfacing consensus opinions within its own population. But that's exactly the problem. When your information diet comes from a handful of curated communities, you don't get the full picture — you get the picture that a particular tribe agreed on. You miss the signal that's sitting in an SEC filing, a patent application, a niche academic preprint, or a government dataset that nobody on Reddit is talking about.

The Idea

The essence of business — of good decision-making in general — is finding information asymmetry. Knowing something that most people don't know yet, and acting on it before the window closes.

So I asked myself: what if instead of scraping 5 sources really well, I built a system that listens to 100+ sources across every corner of the internet? Not just social media. Government filings. Dark pool data. Patent offices. Academic preprints. Package registries. Consumer demand signals. Climate data. Flight tracking. All of it.

The goal isn't to read all of this myself. That's impossible. The goal is to build a pipeline that collects everything, mathematically filters the noise, and surfaces only the moments where something unusual is happening that most people haven't noticed yet.

Why Breadth Beats Depth

When you monitor only Reddit and Twitter, you're seeing what 100K+ people have already seen. By definition, there's no information edge there. But when a niche government dataset shows an anomaly and a specialized industry source corroborates it — while mainstream platforms are silent — that's a window. That's the gap between "a few hundred people know" and "everyone knows."

The system I built tiers every source by how many eyeballs are on it. A FINRA dark pool report is Tier 1 — maybe a few hundred analysts read it. A Reddit front page post is Tier 3 — millions see it. When Tier 1 sources light up and Tier 3 is quiet, you're looking at genuine information asymmetry.

A System That Evolves

The most interesting property of this pipeline is that it gets better the more I use it. Every time I realize I'm missing a perspective, I add a new source. Every false positive teaches the noise filter what to ignore. Every confirmed signal reinforces the patterns worth watching.

It started with 30 scrapers. Now it's past 100. The architecture is designed so adding a new source is trivial — the system auto-discovers it and integrates it into the full processing pipeline. The more I invest in it, the wider my antenna becomes, and the harder it is for important signals to slip through undetected.

Most tools try to help you process information faster. This one helps me see information that others aren't looking at in the first place. That's the difference between efficiency and edge.

I'm not trying to build the next Bloomberg terminal. I'm building a personal listening system that ensures I'm never the last to know — about anything.
